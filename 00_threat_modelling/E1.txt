Threat modelling is a term applied to the techniques that are used to model and analyse technology systems and services to better understand how that system or service might be attacked or otherwise fail, and the measures or controls needed to manage the risk posed by such attacks or failings.

Book: A threat model identifies threats, threat agents, and attack vectors that the target system considers in scope to defend againstâ€”known from the past, or anticipated. Those considered out of scope should be explicitly recorded as such. Threat modeling takes into account adversary modeling (Section 1.4), and should identify and consider all assumptions made about the target system, environment, and attackers. Threat modeling can be done by several different approaches (figure 1.5), as discussed in the subsections below.


Threat modelling exercises:

Example 1 (Online trading platform):

Attacker (who, goal, where):
malicious insider, cybercriminal, spooks (law enforcement, intel), security researchers; all STRIDE goals

Attack Surface:
UI, backend, software stack, APIs, traffic, databases, users, employees, mobile app, devices

Policies: integrity, authorisation, authentication, confidentiality, availability, non-repudiation

Mechanisms: access control, encryption (in transit, at rest), backup, digital signatures, rate limiting, DoS mitigations


Example 2 (IOT pacemaker):

Attacker (who, goal, where):
spooks, terrorists, security researcher, cybercriminals (swamp); DoS, tampering, information disclosure, privilation escalation

Attack Surface:
supply chain / physical devices, firmware, wireless comms, cloud / backend / databases

Policies:
confidentiality / privacy, availability, integrity, (medical) compliance

Mechanisms:
lightweight operation, data encryption, local data storage, user authentication, access control
